<p>A <b>local area network</b> (<b>LAN</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer network</a> that interconnects computers within a limited area such as a residence, campus, or building,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and has its <a href="/wiki/Network_equipment" class="mw-redirect" title="Network equipment">network equipment</a> and interconnects locally managed. LANs facilitate the distribution of data and sharing network devices, such as printers.
</p><p>The LAN contrasts the <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_network" title="Wide area network">wide area network</a> (WAN), which not only covers a larger geographic distance, but also generally involves <a href="/wiki/Leased_line" title="Leased line">leased telecommunication circuits</a> or <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> links. An even greater contrast is the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>, which is a system of globally connected business and personal computers.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> are the two most common technologies used for local area networks; historical network technologies include <a href="/wiki/ARCNET" title="ARCNET">ARCNET</a>, <a href="/wiki/Token_Ring" title="Token Ring">Token Ring</a>, and <a href="/wiki/LocalTalk" title="LocalTalk">LocalTalk</a>.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Cabling">Cabling</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cabling"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:RJ-45-Stecker-und-Buechse.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/RJ-45-Stecker-und-Buechse.jpg/250px-RJ-45-Stecker-und-Buechse.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="175" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/RJ-45-Stecker-und-Buechse.jpg/500px-RJ-45-Stecker-und-Buechse.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="2500" data-file-height="1748" /></a><figcaption>Twisted pair LAN cable</figcaption></figure>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/LAN_cable" class="mw-redirect" title="LAN cable">LAN cable</a></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_over_twisted_pair" title="Ethernet over twisted pair">Ethernet over twisted pair</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twisted_Pair" class="mw-redirect" title="Twisted Pair">Twisted Pair</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a></div>
<p>Most wired network infrastructures utilize <a href="/wiki/Category_5_cable" title="Category 5 cable">Category 5</a> or <a href="/wiki/Category_6_cable" title="Category 6 cable">Category 6</a> <i>twisted pair</i> cabling with <a href="/wiki/RJ45_(telecommunications)" class="mw-redirect" title="RJ45 (telecommunications)">RJ45</a> compatible terminations. This medium provides physical connectivity between the <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> interfaces present on a large number of IP-aware devices. Depending on the grade of cable and quality of installation, speeds of up to 10&#160;Mbit/s, 100&#160;Mbit/s, 1&#160;Gbit/s, or 10&#160;Gbit/s are supported.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Wireless_LAN">Wireless LAN</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wireless LAN"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11" title="IEEE 802.11">IEEE 802.11</a></div>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Wireless_LAN" title="Wireless LAN">wireless LAN</a>, users have unrestricted movement within the coverage area. Wireless networks have become popular in residences and small businesses because of their ease of installation, convenience, and flexibility.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Most wireless LANs consist of devices containing <a href="/wiki/Wireless" title="Wireless">wireless</a> radio technology that conforms to <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11" title="IEEE 802.11">802.11</a> standards as certified by the <a href="/wiki/IEEE" class="mw-redirect" title="IEEE">IEEE</a>. Most wireless-capable residential devices operate at both the 2.4 <a href="/wiki/GHz" class="mw-redirect" title="GHz">GHz</a> and 5 GHz frequencies and fall within the 802.11n or 802.11ac standards.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some older home networking devices operate exclusively at a frequency of 2.4 GHz under 802.11b and 802.11g, or 5 GHz under 802.11a. Some newer devices operate at the aforementioned frequencies in addition to 6 GHz under <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi_6" title="Wi-Fi 6">Wi-Fi 6E</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> is a marketing and compliance certification for IEEE 802.11 technologies.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-wifi&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wifi-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi_Alliance" title="Wi-Fi Alliance">Wi-Fi Alliance</a> has tested compliant products, and certifies them for interoperability. The technology may be integrated into <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet computers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">laptops</a>. Guests are often offered <a href="/wiki/Internet_access" title="Internet access">Internet access</a> via a <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(Wi-Fi)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hotspot (Wi-Fi)">hotspot</a> service.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;" class=""></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Infrastructure_and_technicals">Infrastructure and technicals</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Infrastructure and technicals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Simple_LAN_example_diagram_in_two_rooms.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Simple_LAN_example_diagram_in_two_rooms.png/250px-Simple_LAN_example_diagram_in_two_rooms.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="168" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Simple_LAN_example_diagram_in_two_rooms.png/500px-Simple_LAN_example_diagram_in_two_rooms.png 1.5x" data-file-width="923" data-file-height="620" /></a><figcaption>A conceptual diagram of a LAN at a small business office; this example includes two rooms, each with a <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">switch</a>, as well as a <a href="/wiki/File_server" title="File server">file server</a>, and a mix of wired and wireless connections. This is the <a href="/wiki/Star_topology" class="mw-redirect" title="Star topology">star topology</a>.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Simple LANs in office or school buildings generally consist of cabling and one or more <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">network switches</a>; a switch is used to allow devices on a LAN to talk to one another via <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a>. A switch can be connected to a <a href="/wiki/Router_(computing)" title="Router (computing)">router</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cable_modem" title="Cable modem">cable modem</a>, or <a href="/wiki/ADSL_modem" class="mw-redirect" title="ADSL modem">ADSL modem</a> for <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> access. LANs at residential homes usually tend to have a single router and often may include a <a href="/wiki/Wireless_repeater" title="Wireless repeater">wireless repeater</a>. A LAN can include a wide variety of other network devices such as <a href="/wiki/Firewall_(computing)" title="Firewall (computing)">firewalls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Load_balancing_(computing)" title="Load balancing (computing)">load balancers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Network_intrusion_detection_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Network intrusion detection system">network intrusion detection</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Wireless_access_point" title="Wireless access point">wireless access point</a> is required for connecting wireless devices to a network; when a router includes this device, it is referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Wireless_router" title="Wireless router">wireless router</a>.
</p><p>Advanced LANs are characterized by their use of redundant links with switches using the <a href="/wiki/Spanning_Tree_Protocol" title="Spanning Tree Protocol">Spanning Tree Protocol</a> to prevent loops, their ability to manage differing traffic types via <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_service" title="Quality of service">quality of service</a> (QoS), and their ability to segregate traffic with <a href="/wiki/VLAN" title="VLAN">VLANs</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Network_bridge" title="Network bridge">network bridge</a> binds two different LANs or LAN segments to each other, often in order to grant a wired-only device access to a wireless network medium. 
</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Network_topology" title="Network topology">Network topology</a> describes the layout of interconnections between devices and network segments. At the <a href="/wiki/Data_link_layer" title="Data link layer">data link layer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical layer</a>, a wide variety of LAN topologies have been used, including <a href="/wiki/Ring_network" title="Ring network">ring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bus_network" title="Bus network">bus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesh_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesh network">mesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Star_network" title="Star network">star</a>. The star topology is the most common in contemporary times. Wireless LAN (WLAN) also has its topologies: independent basic service set (IBSS, an <a href="/wiki/Wireless_ad_hoc_network" title="Wireless ad hoc network">ad-hoc network</a>) where each node connects directly to each other (this is also standardized as <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi_Direct" title="Wi-Fi Direct">Wi-Fi Direct</a>), or basic service set (BSS, an infrastructure network that uses an <a href="/wiki/Wireless_access_point" title="Wireless access point">wireless access point</a>).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p><figure class="mw-halign-center" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:LAN_topology.gif" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/LAN_topology.gif/500px-LAN_topology.gif" decoding="async" width="400" height="267" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/LAN_topology.gif/731px-LAN_topology.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5a/LAN_topology.gif 2x" data-file-width="731" data-file-height="488" /></a><figcaption>Various topologies that may be used in a centralised wired LAN: star, ring, bus, and tree</figcaption></figure>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Network_layer_configuration">Network layer configuration</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Network layer configuration"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dynamic_Host_Configuration_Protocol" title="Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol">DHCP</a> is used to assign internal IP addresses to members of a local area network. A DHCP server typically runs on the router<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> with end devices as its clients. All DHCP clients request configuration settings using the DHCP protocol in order to acquire their <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Default_route" title="Default route">default route</a> and one or more <a href="/wiki/DNS_server" class="mw-redirect" title="DNS server">DNS server</a> addresses. Once the client implements these settings, it will be able to communicate on that <a href="/wiki/Internetwork" class="mw-redirect" title="Internetwork">internet</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Protocols">Protocols</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Protocols"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>At the higher network layers, protocols such as <a href="/wiki/NetBIOS" title="NetBIOS">NetBIOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/IPX/SPX" title="IPX/SPX">IPX/SPX</a>, <a href="/wiki/AppleTalk" title="AppleTalk">AppleTalk</a> and others were once common, but the <a href="/wiki/Internet_protocol_suite" title="Internet protocol suite">Internet protocol suite</a> (TCP/IP) has prevailed as the standard of choice for almost all local area networks today.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Connection_to_other_LANs">Connection to other LANs</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Connection to other LANs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>LANs can maintain connections with other LANs via leased lines, leased services, or across the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> using <a href="/wiki/Virtual_private_network" title="Virtual private network">virtual private network</a> technologies. Depending on how the connections are established and secured, and the distance involved, such linked LANs may also be classified as a <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area_network" title="Metropolitan area network">metropolitan area network</a> (MAN) or a <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_network" title="Wide area network">wide area network</a> (WAN).
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Connection_to_the_Internet">Connection to the Internet</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Connection to the Internet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Local area networks may be connected to the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> (a type of <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_network" title="Wide area network">WAN</a>) via fixed-line means (such as a <a href="/wiki/DSL" class="mw-redirect" title="DSL">DSL</a>/<a href="/wiki/ADSL" title="ADSL">ADSL</a> modem<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>) or alternatively using a cellular or satellite <a href="/wiki/Modem" title="Modem">modem</a>. These would additionally make use of telephone wires such as <a href="/wiki/Very_High_Speed_Digital_Subscriber_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Very High Speed Digital Subscriber Line">VDSL</a> and <a href="/wiki/Very_High_Speed_Digital_Subscriber_Line_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Very High Speed Digital Subscriber Line 2">VDSL2</a>, coaxial cables, or <a href="/wiki/Fiber_to_the_home" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiber to the home">fiber to the home</a> for running fiber-optic cables directly into a house or office building, or alternatively a cellular modem or <a href="/wiki/Satellite_dish" title="Satellite dish">satellite dish</a> in the latter non-fixed cases. With <a href="/wiki/Internet_access" title="Internet access">Internet access</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">Internet service provider (ISP)</a> would grant a single WAN-facing <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a> to the network. A router is configured with the provider's IP address on the WAN interface, which is shared among all devices in the LAN by <a href="/wiki/Network_address_translation" title="Network address translation">network address translation</a>. 
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Residential_gateway" title="Residential gateway">gateway</a> establishes <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Data_link_layer" title="Data link layer">data link layer</a> connectivity to a WAN over a service provider's native telecommunications infrastructure. Such devices typically contain a <a href="/wiki/Cable_modem" title="Cable modem">cable</a>, <a href="/wiki/DSL_modem" title="DSL modem">DSL</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Modem#Optical_modem" title="Modem">optical modem</a> bound to a <a href="/wiki/Network_interface_controller" title="Network interface controller">network interface controller</a> for Ethernet. Home and small business class routers are often incorporated into these devices for additional convenience, and they often also have integrated <a href="/wiki/Wireless_access_point" title="Wireless access point">wireless access point</a> and 4-port Ethernet <a href="/wiki/LAN_switching" class="mw-redirect" title="LAN switching">switch</a>. 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/ITU-T" title="ITU-T">ITU-T</a> <a href="/wiki/G.hn" title="G.hn">G.hn</a> and <a href="/wiki/IEEE" class="mw-redirect" title="IEEE">IEEE</a> <a href="/wiki/Power_line_communication" class="mw-redirect" title="Power line communication">Powerline</a> standard, which provide high-speed (up to 1&#160;Gbit/s) local area networking over existing home wiring, are examples of home networking technology designed specifically for <a href="/wiki/Internet_Protocol_television" title="Internet Protocol television">IPTV</a> delivery.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-BERGU142&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BERGU142-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Stay_on_topic" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles"><span title="The material near this tag may contain information that is not relevant to the article&#39;s main topic. (December 2024)">relevant?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

</tr><tr><td class="sidebar-navbar"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1333133064" /><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1239400231" /><div class="navbar plainlinks hlist navbar-mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Home_networking_standards" title="Template:Home networking standards"><abbr title="View this template">v</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-talk"><a href="/wiki/Template_talk:Home_networking_standards" title="Template talk:Home networking standards"><abbr title="Discuss this template">t</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-edit"><a href="/wiki/Special:EditPage/Template:Home_networking_standards" title="Special:EditPage/Template:Home networking standards"><abbr title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The increasing demand and usage of computers in universities and research labs in the late 1960s generated the need to provide high-speed interconnections between computer systems. A 1970 report from the <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Radiation_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence Radiation Laboratory">Lawrence Radiation Laboratory</a> detailing the growth of their "Octopus" network gave a good indication of the situation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A number of experimental and early commercial LAN technologies were developed in the 1970s. <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> was developed at <a href="/wiki/Xerox_PARC" class="mw-redirect" title="Xerox PARC">Xerox PARC</a> between 1973 and 1974.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-metcalfe&#95;video&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metcalfe_video-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Ring_(computer_network)" title="Cambridge Ring (computer network)">Cambridge Ring</a> was developed at Cambridge University starting in 1974.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/ARCNET" title="ARCNET">ARCNET</a> was developed by <a href="/wiki/Datapoint" title="Datapoint">Datapoint</a> Corporation in 1976 and announced in 1977.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It had the first commercial installation in December 1977 at <a href="/wiki/Chase_Manhattan_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Chase Manhattan Bank">Chase Manhattan Bank</a> in New York.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1979,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-European&#95;Parliament&#95;Archives-2021&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-European_Parliament_Archives-2021-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_voting_in_the_European_Parliament" title="Electronic voting in the European Parliament">electronic voting system for the European Parliament</a> was the first installation of a LAN connecting hundreds (420) of microprocessor-controlled voting terminals to a polling/selecting central unit with a <a href="/wiki/Multidrop_bus" title="Multidrop bus">multidrop bus</a> with <a href="/wiki/Master/slave_(technology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Master/slave (technology)">Master/slave (technology)</a> arbitration.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (January 2021)">dubious</span></a>&#32;&#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Local_area_network#Claim_to_first_LAN/on_of_first_LANs_for_Electronic_voting_systems_for_the_European_Parliament" title="Talk:Local area network">discuss</a></i>&#93;</sup> It used 10 kilometers of simple <a href="/wiki/Unshielded_twisted_pair" class="mw-redirect" title="Unshielded twisted pair">unshielded twisted pair</a> <a href="/wiki/Category_3_cable" title="Category 3 cable">category 3 cable</a>—the same cable used for telephone systems—installed inside the benches of the European Parliament  Hemicycles in Strasbourg and Luxembourg.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The development and proliferation of <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computers</a> using the <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">CP/M</a> operating system in the late 1970s, and later <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a>-based systems starting in 1981, meant that many sites grew to dozens or even hundreds of computers. The initial driving force for networking was to share <a href="/wiki/Computer_data_storage" title="Computer data storage">storage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Printer_(computing)" title="Printer (computing)">printers</a>, both of which were expensive at the time. There was much enthusiasm for the concept, and for several years, from about 1983 onward, computer industry pundits habitually declared the coming year to be, "The year of the LAN".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Competing_standards">Competing standards</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Competing standards"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In practice, the concept was marred by the proliferation of incompatible <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical layer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Network_protocol" class="mw-redirect" title="Network protocol">network protocol</a> implementations, and a plethora of methods of sharing resources. Typically, each vendor would have its own type of network card, cabling, protocol, and <a href="/wiki/Network_operating_system" title="Network operating system">network operating system</a>. A solution appeared with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Novell_NetWare" class="mw-redirect" title="Novell NetWare">Novell NetWare</a> which provided even-handed support for dozens of competing card and cable types, and a much more sophisticated operating system than most of its competitors.
</p><p>Of the competitors to NetWare, only <a href="/wiki/Banyan_Vines" class="mw-redirect" title="Banyan Vines">Banyan Vines</a> had comparable technical strengths, but Banyan never gained a secure base. <a href="/wiki/3Com" title="3Com">3Com</a> produced <a href="/wiki/3%2BShare" title="3+Share">3+Share</a> and Microsoft produced <a href="/wiki/MS-Net" title="MS-Net">MS-Net</a>. These then formed the basis for collaboration between <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> and 3Com to create a simple network operating system <a href="/wiki/LAN_Manager" title="LAN Manager">LAN Manager</a> and its cousin, IBM's <a href="/wiki/LAN_Server" class="mw-redirect" title="LAN Server">LAN Server</a>. None of these enjoyed any lasting success; Netware dominated the personal computer LAN business from early after its introduction in 1983 until the mid-1990s when Microsoft introduced <a href="/wiki/Windows_NT" title="Windows NT">Windows NT</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 1983, TCP/IP was first shown capable of supporting actual defense department applications on a Defense Communication Agency LAN testbed located at Reston, Virginia.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The TCP/IP-based LAN successfully supported <a href="/wiki/Telnet" title="Telnet">Telnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/FTP" class="mw-redirect" title="FTP">FTP</a>, and a Defense Department teleconferencing application.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This demonstrated the feasibility of employing TCP/IP LANs to interconnect <a href="/wiki/Worldwide_Military_Command_and_Control_System" title="Worldwide Military Command and Control System">Worldwide Military Command and Control System</a> (WWMCCS) computers at command centers throughout the United States.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, WWMCCS was superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Global_Command_and_Control_System" title="Global Command and Control System">Global Command and Control System</a> (GCCS) before that could happen.
</p><p>During the same period, <a href="/wiki/Unix_workstation" class="mw-redirect" title="Unix workstation">Unix workstations</a> were using TCP/IP networking. Although the workstation market segment is now much reduced, the technologies developed in the area continue to be influential on the Internet and in all forms of networking—and the TCP/IP protocol has replaced <a href="/wiki/IPX" class="mw-redirect" title="IPX">IPX</a>, <a href="/wiki/AppleTalk" title="AppleTalk">AppleTalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/NetBIOS_Frames" class="mw-redirect" title="NetBIOS Frames">NBF</a>, and other protocols used by the early PC LANs.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Econet" title="Econet">Econet</a> was Acorn Computers's low-cost local area network system, intended for use by schools and small businesses. It was first developed for the <a href="/wiki/Acorn_Atom" title="Acorn Atom">Acorn Atom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acorn_System_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Acorn System 2">Acorn System 2</a>/<a href="/wiki/Acorn_System_3" class="mw-redirect" title="Acorn System 3">3</a>/<a href="/wiki/Acorn_System_4" class="mw-redirect" title="Acorn System 4">4</a> computers in 1981.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Further_development">Further development</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Further development"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the 1980s, several token ring network implementations for LANs were developed.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> IBM released its own implementation of token ring in 1985,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It ran at <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7006400000000000000♠"></span>4&#160;<a href="/wiki/Megabit_per_second" class="mw-redirect" title="Megabit per second">Mbit/s</a></span>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> IBM claimed that their token ring systems were superior to Ethernet, especially under load, but these claims were debated;<sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Boggs1988&#95;38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs1988-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> while the slow but inexpensive AppleTalk was popular for Macs, in 1987 <i><a href="/wiki/InfoWorld" title="InfoWorld">InfoWorld</a></i> said, "No LAN has stood out as the clear leader, even in the IBM world".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-satchell19870817&#95;39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-satchell19870817-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> IBM's implementation of token ring was the basis of the IEEE 802.5 standard.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A 16&#160;Mbit/s version of Token Ring was standardized by the 802.5 working group in 1989.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> IBM had market dominance over Token Ring, for example, in 1990, IBM equipment was the most widely used for Token Ring networks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fiber_Distributed_Data_Interface" title="Fiber Distributed Data Interface">Fiber Distributed Data Interface</a> (FDDI), a LAN standard, was considered an attractive campus <a href="/wiki/Backbone_network" title="Backbone network">backbone network</a> technology in the early to mid 1990s since existing Ethernet networks only offered 10&#160;Mbit/s data rates and Token Ring networks only offered 4&#160;Mbit/s or 16&#160;Mbit/s rates. Thus it was a relatively high-speed choice of that era, with speeds such as 100&#160;Mbit/s.
By 1994, vendors included <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Cisco Systems">Cisco Systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Semiconductor" title="National Semiconductor">National Semiconductor</a>, Network Peripherals, SysKonnect (acquired by <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Marvell Technology Group">Marvell Technology Group</a>), and <a href="/wiki/3Com" title="3Com">3Com</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> FDDI installations have largely been replaced by Ethernet deployments.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
