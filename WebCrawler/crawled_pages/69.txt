<p><b>Blini</b> (plural <i>blinis</i> or <i>blini</i>, rarely <i>bliny</i>;<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ahd&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ahd-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-macmillan&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macmillan-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">блины</span>; singular: <b>blin</b>) are Russian <a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%AApe" title="Crêpe">crêpes</a>, often made with a yeast-raised batter of buckwheat and/or wheat flour and milk.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bender&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bender-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mason&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Ayto&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ayto-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They may be served with <a href="/wiki/Smetana_(dairy_product)" title="Smetana (dairy product)">smetana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cottage_cheese" title="Cottage cheese">cottage cheese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caviar" title="Caviar">caviar</a> and other garnishes, or simply smeared with <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bender&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bender-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Ayto&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ayto-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They are a traditional dish in <a href="/wiki/Russian_cuisine" title="Russian cuisine">Russian cuisine</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  
</p><p>In the West, blini traditionally refers to small savory <a href="/wiki/Pancake" title="Pancake">pancakes</a> made with leavened batter. In modern Russian, the term most often refers to pan-sized leavened thin pancakes, although smaller leavened pancakes are also called blini. Smaller and thicker pancakes (with several of them baked on one larger pan) are called <i><a href="/wiki/Oladyi" title="Oladyi">oladyi</a></i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Blintzes" class="mw-redirect" title="Blintzes">Blintzes</a>, called <i>blinchiki</i> (little blinis) in Russian, are an offshoot of blini or crêpes. They are basically <a href="/wiki/Roll_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Roll (food)">rolls</a> based on thin pancakes usually made of wheat flour, folded to form a casing for various kinds of filling, typically cheese, fruit, or (in Russian cuisine) pre-fried <a href="/wiki/Minced_meat" class="mw-redirect" title="Minced meat">minced meat</a>, and then sautéed or baked.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Etymology">Etymology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Proto-Slavic_language" title="Proto-Slavic language">Proto-Slavic</a> term for the Russian pancakes was probably <i>mlinŭ</i> ('to mill'), which was transformed in Old Russian into <span title="Old East Slavic-language text"><span lang="orv">млинъ</span></span>, <span title="Old East Slavic-language romanization"><i lang="orv-Latn">mlinŭ</i></span>, and <span title="Old East Slavic-language text"><span lang="orv">блинъ</span></span>, <span title="Old East Slavic-language romanization"><i lang="orv-Latn">blinŭ</i></span> (cf. <span title="Ukrainian-language text"><span lang="uk">млинець</span></span>, <span title="Ukrainian-language romanization"><i lang="uk-Latn">mlynets</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a> for <i>blin</i>).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Max_Vasmer" title="Max Vasmer">Max Vasmer</a>, in his <i>Etymological Dictionary of the Russian Language</i>, notes that a similar word is used in many Slavic languages, as well as in Latvian and Lithuanian.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> While the modern Russian word <span title="Russian-language text"><span lang="ru">блины</span></span>, <span title="Russian-language romanization"><i lang="ru-Latn">bliny</i></span> (plural of <span title="Russian-language text"><span lang="ru">блин</span></span>, <span title="Russian-language romanization"><i lang="ru-Latn">blin</i></span>), also refers to foreign-introduced <a href="/wiki/Pancake" title="Pancake">pancakes</a> in general, the term <span title="Russian-language text"><span lang="ru">русские блины</span></span>, <span title="Russian-language romanization"><i lang="ru-Latn">russkiye bliny</i></span> ('Russian pancakes'), is often emphasized in Russia for differentiation.
</p><p>Some English dictionaries record usage of the forms <i>blin</i> as singular and <i>blini</i> or <i>bliny</i> as plural, which corresponds to the original Russian forms, but other dictionaries consider this usage so rare in English that they do not mention <i>blin</i> at all and only record the widespread modern regular usage of <i>blini</i> for the singular and <i>blinis</i> for the plural.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Ayto&#95;7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ayto-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Blini have a history dating to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mason&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In Russian culture, blini were traditionally prepared at the end of winter during <a href="/wiki/Maslenitsa" title="Maslenitsa">Maslenitsa</a> ('butter week') as a treat eaten twice a day.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mason&#95;6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There were also other occasions for eating blini, such as at funerals and during prayers for the dead.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mason&#95;6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Traditional Russian blini are made with yeasted batter, which is left to rise and then diluted with <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soured_milk" title="Soured milk">soured milk</a>, and cold or boiling water. When diluted with boiling water, they are referred to as <i>zavarnye bliny</i> (the Russian term <i>zavarnoe testo</i> corresponds to "<a href="/wiki/Choux_pastry" title="Choux pastry">choux pastry</a>"). A lighter and thinner form made from unyeasted batter (usually made of flour, eggs, milk, or soured milk, <a href="/wiki/Kefir" title="Kefir">kefir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryazhenka" title="Ryazhenka">ryazhenka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Varenets" title="Varenets">varenets</a>) is also common in Russia.  All kinds of flour may be used, from <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a> to <a href="/wiki/Oatmeal" title="Oatmeal">oatmeal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, although wheat is currently the most popular. Historically, blini have been baked in a <a href="/wiki/Russian_oven" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian oven">Russian oven</a>, which was used for heat processing of all kinds of food. Even though blini are nowadays pan-fried, like pancakes, preparing blini is still referred to as "pech bliny" (i.e., "to bake blini") in Russian, and the word for "Russian oven" is a cognate, "(russkya) pech".
</p><p>The influence of French cuisine in the 19th century may have led to the development of lighter blini.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mason&#95;6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Lesley_Chamberlain" title="Lesley Chamberlain">Lesley Chamberlain</a>: "In a full Russian <i>obed</i>, blini are served after the cold <i><a href="/wiki/Zakuski" title="Zakuski">zakuski</a></i>. They may be followed by <a href="/wiki/Consomm%C3%A9" title="Consommé">consommé</a>, then pies and then the main meat course. For all this you would need a gargantuan appetite..."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mason&#95;6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Varieties">Varieties</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Varieties"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Preparation_of_blins_or_blini.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Preparation_of_blins_or_blini.jpg/250px-Preparation_of_blins_or_blini.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="99" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Preparation_of_blins_or_blini.jpg/500px-Preparation_of_blins_or_blini.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="791" data-file-height="312" /></a><figcaption>The preparation of bliny</figcaption></figure>
<p>Some ways that blini are prepared and served include the following:
</p>
<ul><li>Blini made from <a href="/wiki/Batter_(cooking)" title="Batter (cooking)">batter</a> containing various additions such as grated <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potato</a> or <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raisins" class="mw-redirect" title="Raisins">raisins</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Encyclopedia&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Blini covered with <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sour_cream" title="Sour cream">sour cream</a>, <a href="/wiki/Varenie" class="mw-redirect" title="Varenie">varenie</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jam" class="mw-redirect" title="Jam">jam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> or <a href="/wiki/Caviar" title="Caviar">caviar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Whitefish_(fisheries_term)" title="Whitefish (fisheries term)">whitefish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> or traditional <a href="/wiki/Sturgeon" title="Sturgeon">sturgeon</a>).</li></ul>
<p>They may be folded or rolled into a tube with sweet or salty fillings such as <a href="/wiki/Varenye" title="Varenye">varenye</a>, fruit, berry, mashed potatoes, <a href="/wiki/Tvorog" title="Tvorog">tvorog</a>, cooked <a href="/wiki/Ground_meat" title="Ground meat">ground meat</a>, cooked <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">chicken</a>, salmon, chopped boiled eggs with green onions or chopped mushrooms.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Blini made by pouring batter over chopped vegetables, meat, or mushrooms put on a frying pan beforehand are called "blini s pripyokom."</li>
<li>Caviar is a popular filling for blini during Russian-style parties and in foreign Russian-style restaurants.</li>
<li><b>Buckwheat blini</b> are part of traditional <a href="/wiki/Russian_cuisine" title="Russian cuisine">Russian cuisine</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Encyclopedia&#95;14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They are also widespread in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Encyclopedia&#95;14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> where they are sometimes known as <span title="Ukrainian-language romanization"><i lang="uk-Latn">hrechanyky</i></span> (<a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk">гречаники</span>), and Lithuania's <a href="/wiki/Dz%C5%ABkija" title="Dzūkija">Dzūkija</a> region, the only region of the country in which buckwheat is grown, where they are called <i>grikių blynai</i> (which is in fact literally "buckwheat blini").</li></ul>
<p>Similar pancakes are eaten in neighboring countries, such as <i>nalistniki</i> in Belarus, although blini are also widely eaten.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Aside from referring to pancakes, the word <i><span title="Russian-language romanization"><i lang="ru-Latn">blin</i></span></i> (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">блин</span>) is used in Russian as a "<a href="/wiki/Minced_oath" title="Minced oath">minced oath</a>" for the <a href="/wiki/Russian_mat" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian mat">Russian swear word</a> "блять" <span title="Russian-language romanization"><i lang="ru-Latn">blyat'</i></span>, used as an interjection to express a negative emotion, akin to the words "damn!" or saying "Holy Moly!" while meaning "Holy shit!".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-leru&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leru-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>There are many Russian proverbs involving <i>blini</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For example, "<a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9F%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%B2%D1%8B%D0%B9_%D0%B1%D0%BB%D0%B8%D0%BD_%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%BC%D0%BE%D0%BC" class="extiw" title="ru:Первый блин комом">Первый блин комом</a>" ("The first <i>blin</i> is lumpy") is a figurative saying that the first attempt to do something is expected to be unsuccessful, said to calm down the person who failed the first try.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-leru&#95;17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leru-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> An English equivalent would be "You must spoil before you spin".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-leru&#95;17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leru-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
