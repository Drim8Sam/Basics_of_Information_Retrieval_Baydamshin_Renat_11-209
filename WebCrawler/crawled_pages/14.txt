For biclique attacks on AES-192 and AES-256, the computational complexities of 2<sup>189.7</sup> and 2<sup>254.4</sup> respectively apply. <a href="/wiki/Related-key_attack" title="Related-key attack">Related-key attacks</a> can break AES-192 and AES-256 with complexities 2<sup>99.5</sup> and 2<sup>176</sup> in both time and data, respectively.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-relkey&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-relkey-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
Another attack was blogged<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bruce&#95;Schneier&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruce_Schneier-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and released as a <a href="/wiki/Preprint" title="Preprint">preprint</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> in 2009. This attack is against AES-256 that uses only two related keys and 2<sup>39</sup> time to recover the complete 256-bit key of a 9-round version, or 2<sup>45</sup> time for a 10-round version with a stronger type of related subkey attack, or 2<sup>70</sup> time for an 11-round version.</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The <b>Advanced Encryption Standard</b> (<b>AES</b>), also known by its original name <b>Rijndael</b> (<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1177148991">.mw-parser-output .IPA-label-small{font-size:85%}.mw-parser-output .references .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .infobox .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .navbox .IPA-label-small{font-size:100%}</style><span class="IPA-label IPA-label-small">Dutch pronunciation:</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="nl-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Dutch" title="Help:IPA/Dutch">&#91;ˈrɛindaːl&#93;</a></span>),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rijndael-ammended.pdf&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rijndael-ammended.pdf-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> is a specification for the <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encryption</a> of electronic data established by the US <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST) in 2001.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-fips-197&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fips-197-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>AES is a variant of the Rijndael <a href="/wiki/Block_cipher" title="Block cipher">block cipher</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rijndael-ammended.pdf&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rijndael-ammended.pdf-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> developed by two <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> cryptographers, <a href="/wiki/Joan_Daemen" title="Joan Daemen">Joan Daemen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Rijmen" title="Vincent Rijmen">Vincent Rijmen</a>, who submitted a proposal<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rijndaelv2&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rijndaelv2-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> to NIST during the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Encryption_Standard_process" title="Advanced Encryption Standard process">AES selection process</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Rijndael is a family of ciphers with different <a href="/wiki/Key_size" title="Key size">key</a> and <a href="/wiki/Block_size_(cryptography)" title="Block size (cryptography)">block sizes</a>. For AES, NIST selected three members of the Rijndael family, each with a block size of 128 bits, but three different key lengths: 128, 192 and 256 bits.
</p><p>AES has been adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">US government</a>. It supersedes the <a href="/wiki/Data_Encryption_Standard" title="Data Encryption Standard">Data Encryption Standard</a> (DES),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> which was published in 1977. The algorithm described by AES is a <a href="/wiki/Symmetric-key_algorithm" title="Symmetric-key algorithm">symmetric-key algorithm</a>, meaning the same key is used for both encrypting and decrypting the data.
</p><p>In the United States, AES was announced by the NIST as US <a href="/wiki/Federal_Information_Processing_Standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Information Processing Standard">FIPS</a> PUB 197 (FIPS 197) on November 26, 2001.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-fips-197&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fips-197-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This announcement followed a five-year standardization process in which fifteen competing designs were presented and evaluated, before the Rijndael cipher was selected as the most suitable.
</p><p>AES is included in the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>/<a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">IEC</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_International_Organization_for_Standardization_standards,_18000-19999" class="mw-redirect" title="List of International Organization for Standardization standards, 18000-19999">18033-3</a> standard.  AES became effective as a US federal government standard on May 26, 2002, after approval by US <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Commerce" title="United States Secretary of Commerce">Secretary of Commerce</a> <a href="/wiki/Donald_Evans" title="Donald Evans">Donald Evans</a>. AES is available in many different encryption packages, and is the first (and only) publicly accessible <a href="/wiki/Cipher" title="Cipher">cipher</a> approved by the US <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a> (NSA) for <a href="/wiki/Classified_information" title="Classified information">top secret</a> information when used in an NSA approved cryptographic module. <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033199720">.mw-parser-output div.crossreference{padding-left:0}</style><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable crossreference selfref">(see <a href="#Security">§&#160;Security</a> below)</span>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Definitive_standards">Definitive standards</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitive standards"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) is defined in each of:
</p>
<ul><li>FIPS PUB 197: Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-fips-197&#95;8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fips-197-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>ISO/IEC 18033-3: Block ciphers<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ISO&#95;IEC&#95;AES&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISO_IEC_AES-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Description_of_the_ciphers">Description of the ciphers</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description of the ciphers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>AES is based on a design principle known as a <a href="/wiki/Substitution%E2%80%93permutation_network" title="Substitution–permutation network">substitution–permutation network</a>, and is efficient in both software and hardware.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Unlike its predecessor DES, AES does not use a <a href="/wiki/Feistel_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Feistel network">Feistel network</a>. AES is a variant of Rijndael, with a fixed <a href="/wiki/Block_size_(cryptography)" title="Block size (cryptography)">block size</a> of 128 <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bits</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Key_size" title="Key size">key size</a> of 128, 192, or 256 bits. By contrast, Rijndael <i>per se</i> is specified with block and key sizes that may be any multiple of 32 bits, with a minimum of 128 and a maximum of 256 bits. Most AES calculations are done in a particular <a href="/wiki/Finite_field_arithmetic" title="Finite field arithmetic">finite field</a>.
</p><p>AES operates on a 4 × 4 <a href="/wiki/Column-major_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Column-major order">column-major order</a> array of 16 bytes <span class="texhtml"><i>b</i><sub>0</sub>, <i>b</i><sub>1</sub>, ..., <i>b</i><sub>15</sub></span> termed the <i>state</i>:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>note 3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>

</p><p>The key size used for an AES cipher specifies the number of transformation rounds that convert the input, called the <a href="/wiki/Plaintext" title="Plaintext">plaintext</a>, into the final output, called the <a href="/wiki/Ciphertext" title="Ciphertext">ciphertext</a>. The number of rounds are as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>10 rounds for 128-bit keys;</li>
<li>12 rounds for 192-bit keys;</li>
<li>14 rounds for 256-bit keys.</li></ul>
<p>Each round consists of several processing steps, including one that depends on the encryption key itself. A set of reverse rounds are applied to transform ciphertext back into the original plaintext using the same encryption key.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="High-level_description_of_the_algorithm">High-level description of the algorithm</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: High-level description of the algorithm"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<ol><li><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886049734">.mw-parser-output .monospaced{font-family:monospace,monospace}</style><span class="monospaced"> KeyExpansion</span>&#160;&#8211;&#32;round keys are derived from the cipher key using the <a href="/wiki/AES_key_schedule" title="AES key schedule">AES key schedule</a>. AES requires a separate 128-bit round key block for each round plus one more.</li>
<li>Initial round key addition:
<ol><li><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> AddRoundKey</span>&#160;&#8211;&#32;each byte of the state is combined with a byte of the round key using <a href="/wiki/Bitwise_xor" class="mw-redirect" title="Bitwise xor">bitwise xor</a>.</li></ol></li>
<li>9, 11 or 13 rounds:
<ol><li><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> SubBytes</span>&#160;&#8211;&#32;a <a href="/wiki/Linear_map" title="Linear map">non-linear</a> substitution step where each byte is replaced with another according to a <a href="/wiki/Rijndael_S-box" title="Rijndael S-box">lookup table</a>.</li>
<li><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> ShiftRows</span>&#160;&#8211;&#32;a transposition step where the last three rows of the state are shifted cyclically a certain number of steps.</li>
<li><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> MixColumns</span>&#160;&#8211;&#32;a linear mixing operation which operates on the columns of the state, combining the four bytes in each column.</li>
<li><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> AddRoundKey</span></li></ol></li>
<li>Final round (making 10, 12 or 14 rounds in total):
<ol><li><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> SubBytes</span></li>
<li><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> ShiftRows</span></li>
<li><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> AddRoundKey</span></li></ol></li></ol>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="The_SubBytes_step">The <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced">SubBytes</span> step</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The SubBytes step"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rijndael_S-box" title="Rijndael S-box">Rijndael S-box</a></div>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:AES-SubBytes.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/AES-SubBytes.svg/330px-AES-SubBytes.svg.png" decoding="async" width="320" height="166" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/AES-SubBytes.svg/500px-AES-SubBytes.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/AES-SubBytes.svg/960px-AES-SubBytes.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="810" data-file-height="420" /></a><figcaption>In the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> SubBytes</span> step, each byte in the state is replaced with its entry in a fixed 8-bit lookup table, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">S</span>; <span class="texhtml"><i>b<sub>ij</sub></i> = <i>S</i>(<i>a<sub>ij</sub></i>)</span>.</figcaption></figure>
<p>In the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> SubBytes</span> step, each byte <span class="texhtml"><i>a<sub>i,j</sub></i></span> in the <i>state</i> array is replaced with a <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> SubByte</span> <span class="texhtml"><i>S</i>(<i>a<sub>i,j</sub></i>)</span> using an 8-bit <a href="/wiki/Substitution_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Substitution box">substitution box</a>. Before round 0, the <i>state</i> array is simply the plaintext/input. This operation provides the non-linearity in the <a href="/wiki/Cipher" title="Cipher">cipher</a>. The S-box used is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse" title="Multiplicative inverse">multiplicative inverse</a> over <span class="texhtml"><a href="/wiki/Finite_field" title="Finite field">GF</a>(2<sup>8</sup>)</span>, known to have good non-linearity properties. To avoid attacks based on simple algebraic properties, the S-box is constructed by combining the inverse function with an invertible <a href="/wiki/Affine_transformation" title="Affine transformation">affine transformation</a>. The S-box is also chosen to avoid any fixed points (and so is a <a href="/wiki/Derangement" title="Derangement">derangement</a>), i.e., <span class="texhtml"><i>S</i>(<i>a<sub>i,j</sub></i>) ≠ <i>a<sub>i,j</sub></i></span>, and also any opposite fixed points, i.e., <span class="texhtml"><i>S</i>(<i>a<sub>i,j</sub></i>) ⊕ <i>a<sub>i,j</sub></i> ≠ FF<sub>16</sub></span>.
While performing the decryption, the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> InvSubBytes</span> step (the inverse of <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> SubBytes</span>) is used, which requires first taking the inverse of the affine transformation and then finding the multiplicative inverse.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="The_ShiftRows_step">The <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced">ShiftRows</span> step</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The ShiftRows step"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:AES-ShiftRows.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/AES-ShiftRows.svg/330px-AES-ShiftRows.svg.png" decoding="async" width="320" height="119" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/AES-ShiftRows.svg/500px-AES-ShiftRows.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/AES-ShiftRows.svg/960px-AES-ShiftRows.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="810" data-file-height="300" /></a><figcaption>In the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> ShiftRows</span> step, bytes in each row of the state are shifted cyclically to the left. The number of places each byte is shifted differs incrementally for each row.</figcaption></figure>
<p>The <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> ShiftRows</span> step operates on the rows of the state; it cyclically shifts the bytes in each row by a certain <a href="/wiki/Offset_(computer_science)" title="Offset (computer science)">offset</a>. For AES, the first row is left unchanged. Each byte of the second row is shifted one to the left. Similarly, the third and fourth rows are shifted by offsets of two and three respectively.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>note 4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In this way, each column of the output state of the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> ShiftRows</span> step is composed of bytes from each column of the input state. The importance of this step is to avoid the columns being encrypted independently, in which case AES would degenerate into four independent block ciphers.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="The_MixColumns_step">The <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced">MixColumns</span> step</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The MixColumns step"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rijndael_MixColumns" title="Rijndael MixColumns">Rijndael MixColumns</a></div>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:AES-MixColumns.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/AES-MixColumns.svg/330px-AES-MixColumns.svg.png" decoding="async" width="320" height="170" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/AES-MixColumns.svg/500px-AES-MixColumns.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/AES-MixColumns.svg/960px-AES-MixColumns.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="810" data-file-height="430" /></a><figcaption>In the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> MixColumns</span> step, each column of the state is multiplied with a fixed polynomial <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i>(<i>x</i>)</span>.</figcaption></figure>
<p>In the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> MixColumns</span> step, the four bytes of each column of the state are combined using an invertible <a href="/wiki/Linear_transformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Linear transformation">linear transformation</a>. The <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> MixColumns</span> function takes four bytes as input and outputs four bytes, where each input byte affects all four output bytes. Together with <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> ShiftRows</span>, <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> MixColumns</span> provides <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_(cryptography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Diffusion (cryptography)">diffusion</a> in the cipher.

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="The_AddRoundKey">The <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced">AddRoundKey</span></h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The AddRoundKey"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:AES-AddRoundKey.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/AES-AddRoundKey.svg/330px-AES-AddRoundKey.svg.png" decoding="async" width="320" height="249" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/AES-AddRoundKey.svg/500px-AES-AddRoundKey.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/AES-AddRoundKey.svg/960px-AES-AddRoundKey.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="810" data-file-height="630" /></a><figcaption>In the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> AddRoundKey</span> step, each byte of the state is combined with a byte of the round subkey using the <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_or" title="Exclusive or">XOR</a> operation (⊕).</figcaption></figure>
<p>In the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> AddRoundKey</span> step, the subkey is combined with the state. For each round, a subkey is derived from the main <a href="/wiki/Key_(cryptography)" title="Key (cryptography)">key</a> using <a href="/wiki/Rijndael_key_schedule" class="mw-redirect" title="Rijndael key schedule">Rijndael's key schedule</a>; each subkey is the same size as the state. The subkey is added by combining of the state with the corresponding byte of the subkey using bitwise <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_or" title="Exclusive or">XOR</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Optimization_of_the_cipher">Optimization of the cipher</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Optimization of the cipher"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>On systems with 32-bit or larger words, it is possible to speed up execution of this cipher by combining the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> SubBytes</span> and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> ShiftRows</span> steps with the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> MixColumns</span> step by transforming them into a sequence of table lookups. This requires four 256-entry 32-bit tables (together occupying 4096 bytes).  A round can then be performed with 16 table lookup operations and 12 32-bit exclusive-or operations, followed by four 32-bit exclusive-or operations in the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> AddRoundKey</span> step.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  Alternatively, the table lookup operation can be performed with a single 256-entry 32-bit table (occupying 1024 bytes) followed by circular rotation operations.
</p><p>Using a byte-oriented approach, it is possible to combine the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> SubBytes</span>, <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> ShiftRows</span>, and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734" /><span class="monospaced"> MixColumns</span> steps into a single round operation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Security">Security</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Security"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a> (NSA) reviewed all the AES finalists, including Rijndael, and stated that all of them were secure enough for US Government non-classified data. In June 2003, the US Government announced that AES could be used to protect <a href="/wiki/Classified_information" title="Classified information">classified information</a>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The design and strength of all key lengths of the AES algorithm (i.e., 128, 192 and 256) are sufficient to protect classified information up to the SECRET level. TOP SECRET information will require use of either the 192 or 256 key lengths. The implementation of AES in products intended to protect national security systems and/or information must be reviewed and certified by NSA prior to their acquisition and use.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>AES has 10 rounds for 128-bit keys, 12 rounds for 192-bit keys, and 14 rounds for 256-bit keys.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Known_attacks">Known attacks</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Known attacks"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>For cryptographers, a <a href="/wiki/Cryptanalysis" title="Cryptanalysis">cryptographic</a> "break" is anything faster than a <a href="/wiki/Brute-force_attack" title="Brute-force attack">brute-force attack</a>&#160;&#8211;&#32; i.e., performing one trial decryption for each possible key in sequence <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033199720" /><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable crossreference">(see <a href="/wiki/Cryptanalysis#Computational_resources_required" title="Cryptanalysis">Cryptanalysis §&#160;Computational resources required</a>)</span>. A break can thus include results that are infeasible with current technology. Despite being impractical, theoretical breaks can sometimes provide insight into vulnerability patterns. The largest successful publicly known brute-force attack against a widely implemented block-cipher encryption algorithm was against a 64-bit <a href="/wiki/RC5" title="RC5">RC5</a> key by <a href="/wiki/Distributed.net" title="Distributed.net">distributed.net</a> in 2006.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ZD20060430&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZD20060430-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The key space increases by a factor of 2 for each additional bit of key length, and if every possible value of the key is equiprobable; this translates into a doubling of the average brute-force key search time with every additional bit of key length. This implies that the effort of a brute-force search increases exponentially with key length. Key length in itself does not imply security against attacks, since there are ciphers with very long keys that have been found to be vulnerable.
</p><p>AES has a fairly simple algebraic framework.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2002, a theoretical attack, named the "<a href="/wiki/XSL_attack" title="XSL attack">XSL attack</a>", was announced by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Courtois" title="Nicolas Courtois">Nicolas Courtois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Josef_Pieprzyk" title="Josef Pieprzyk">Josef Pieprzyk</a>, purporting to show a weakness in the AES algorithm, partially due to the low complexity of its nonlinear components.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Since then, other papers have shown that the attack, as originally presented, is unworkable; see <a href="/wiki/XSL_attack#Application_to_block_ciphers" title="XSL attack">XSL attack on block ciphers</a>.
</p><p>During the AES selection process, developers of competing algorithms wrote of Rijndael's algorithm "we are concerned about [its] use&#160;... in security-critical applications."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-rijndael-algebraic&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rijndael-algebraic-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In October 2000, however, at the end of the AES selection process, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Schneier" title="Bruce Schneier">Bruce Schneier</a>, a developer of the competing algorithm <a href="/wiki/Twofish" title="Twofish">Twofish</a>, wrote that while he thought successful academic attacks on Rijndael would be developed someday, he "did not believe that anyone will ever discover an attack that will allow someone to read Rijndael traffic."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>By 2006, the best known attacks were on 7 rounds for 128-bit keys, 8 rounds for 192-bit keys, and 9 rounds for 256-bit keys.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-improved&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-improved-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Until May 2009, the only successful published attacks against the full AES were <a href="/wiki/Side-channel_attack" title="Side-channel attack">side-channel attacks</a> on some specific implementations. In 2009, a new <a href="/wiki/Related-key_attack" title="Related-key attack">related-key attack</a> was discovered that exploits the simplicity of AES's key schedule and has a complexity of 2<sup>119</sup>. In December 2009 it was improved to 2<sup>99.5</sup>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-relkey&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-relkey-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This is a follow-up to an attack discovered earlier in 2009 by <a href="/wiki/Alex_Biryukov" title="Alex Biryukov">Alex Biryukov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Khovratovich" title="Dmitry Khovratovich">Dmitry Khovratovich</a>, and Ivica Nikolić, with a complexity of 2<sup>96</sup> for one out of every 2<sup>35</sup> keys.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, related-key attacks are not of concern in any properly designed cryptographic protocol, as a properly designed protocol (i.e., implementational software) will take care not to allow related keys, essentially by <a href="/wiki/Related-key_attack#Preventing_related-key_attacks" title="Related-key attack">constraining</a> an attacker's means of selecting keys for relatedness.
</p><p>Another attack was blogged by Bruce Schneier<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bruce&#95;Schneier&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruce_Schneier-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
on July 30, 2009, and released as a <a href="/wiki/Preprint" title="Preprint">preprint</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
on August 3, 2009. This new attack, by Alex Biryukov, <a href="/wiki/Orr_Dunkelman" title="Orr Dunkelman">Orr Dunkelman</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathan_Keller&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nathan Keller (page does not exist)">Nathan Keller</a>, Dmitry Khovratovich, and <a href="/wiki/Adi_Shamir" title="Adi Shamir">Adi Shamir</a>, is against AES-256 that uses only two related keys and 2<sup>39</sup> time to recover the complete 256-bit key of a 9-round version, or 2<sup>45</sup> time for a 10-round version with a stronger type of related subkey attack, or 2<sup>70</sup> time for an 11-round version. 256-bit AES uses 14 rounds, so these attacks are not effective against full AES.
</p><p>The practicality of these attacks with stronger related keys has been criticized,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> for instance, by the paper on chosen-key-relations-in-the-middle attacks on AES-128 authored by Vincent Rijmen in 2010.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2009, the first <a href="/wiki/Known-key_distinguishing_attack" title="Known-key distinguishing attack">known-key distinguishing attack</a> against a reduced 8-round version of AES-128 was released as a preprint.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
This known-key distinguishing attack is an improvement of the rebound, or the start-from-the-middle attack, against AES-like permutations, which view two consecutive rounds of permutation as the application of a so-called Super-S-box. It works on the 8-round version of AES-128, with a time complexity of 2<sup>48</sup>, and a memory complexity of 2<sup>32</sup>.  128-bit AES uses 10 rounds, so this attack is not effective against full AES-128.
</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Key-recovery_attack" title="Key-recovery attack">key-recovery attacks</a> on full AES were by Andrey Bogdanov, Dmitry Khovratovich, and Christian Rechberger, and were published in 2011.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The attack is a <a href="/wiki/Biclique_attack" title="Biclique attack">biclique attack</a> and is faster than brute force by a factor of about four. It requires 2<sup>126.2</sup> operations to recover an AES-128 key. For AES-192 and AES-256, 2<sup>190.2</sup> and 2<sup>254.6</sup> operations are needed, respectively. This result has been further improved to 2<sup>126.0</sup> for AES-128, 2<sup>189.9</sup> for AES-192, and 2<sup>254.3</sup> for AES-256 by Biaoshuai Tao and Hongjun Wu in a 2015 paper,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> which are the current best results in key recovery attack against AES.
</p><p>This is a very small gain, as a 126-bit key (instead of 128 bits) would still take billions of years to brute force on current and foreseeable hardware. Also, the authors calculate the best attack using their technique on AES with a 128-bit key requires storing 2<sup>88</sup> bits of data. That works out to about 38 trillion terabytes of data, which was more than all the data stored on all the computers on the planet in 2016.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A paper in 2015 later improved the space complexity to 2<sup>56</sup> bits,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> which is 9007 terabytes (while still keeping a time complexity of approximately 2<sup>126</sup>).
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Edward_Snowden#Surveillance_disclosures" title="Edward Snowden">Snowden documents</a>, the NSA is doing research on whether a cryptographic attack based on <a href="/wiki/Kendall_tau_rank_correlation_coefficient" class="mw-redirect" title="Kendall tau rank correlation coefficient">tau statistic</a> may help to break AES.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>At present, there is no known practical attack that would allow someone without knowledge of the key to read data encrypted by AES when correctly implemented.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Side-channel_attacks">Side-channel attacks</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Side-channel attacks"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Side-channel_attack" title="Side-channel attack">Side-channel attacks</a> do not attack the cipher as a <a href="/wiki/Black_box" title="Black box">black box</a>, and thus are not related to cipher security as defined in the classical context, but are important in practice. They attack implementations of the cipher on hardware or software systems that inadvertently leak data. There are several such known attacks on various implementations of AES.
</p><p>In April 2005, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_J._Bernstein" title="Daniel J. Bernstein">D.&#160;J. Bernstein</a> announced a cache-timing attack that he used to break a custom server that used <a href="/wiki/OpenSSL" title="OpenSSL">OpenSSL</a>'s AES encryption.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-bernstein&#95;timing&#95;34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bernstein_timing-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The attack required over 200 million chosen plaintexts.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The custom server was designed to give out as much timing information as possible (the server reports back the number of machine cycles taken by the encryption operation). However, as Bernstein pointed out, "reducing the precision of the server's timestamps, or eliminating them from the server's responses, does not stop the attack: the client simply uses round-trip timings based on its local clock, and compensates for the increased noise by averaging over a larger number of samples."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-bernstein&#95;timing&#95;34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bernstein_timing-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2005, Dag Arne Osvik, <a href="/wiki/Adi_Shamir" title="Adi Shamir">Adi Shamir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eran_Tromer" title="Eran Tromer">Eran Tromer</a> presented a paper demonstrating several cache-timing attacks against the implementations in AES found in OpenSSL and Linux's <code>dm-crypt</code> partition encryption function.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> One attack was able to obtain an entire AES key after only 800 operations triggering encryptions, in a total of 65 milliseconds. This attack requires the attacker to be able to run programs on the same system or platform that is performing AES.
</p><p>In December 2009 an attack on some hardware implementations was published that used <a href="/wiki/Differential_fault_analysis" title="Differential fault analysis">differential fault analysis</a> and allows recovery of a key with a complexity of 2<sup>32</sup>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2010 Endre Bangerter, David Gullasch and Stephan Krenn published a paper which described a practical approach to a "near real time" recovery of secret keys from AES-128 without the need for either cipher text or plaintext. The approach also works on AES-128 implementations that use compression tables, such as OpenSSL.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Like some earlier attacks, this one requires the ability to run unprivileged code on the system performing the AES encryption, which may be achieved by malware infection far more easily than commandeering the root account.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2016, C. Ashokkumar, Ravi Prakash Giri and Bernard Menezes presented a side-channel attack on AES implementations that can recover the complete 128-bit AES key in just 6–7 blocks of plaintext/ciphertext, which is a substantial improvement over previous works that require between 100 and a million encryptions.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The proposed attack requires standard user privilege and key-retrieval algorithms run under a minute.
</p><p>Many modern CPUs have built-in <a href="/wiki/AES_instruction_set" title="AES instruction set">hardware instructions for AES</a>, which protect against timing-related side-channel attacks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Quantum_attacks">Quantum attacks</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Quantum attacks"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>AES-256 is considered to be <a href="/wiki/Post-quantum_cryptography" title="Post-quantum cryptography">quantum resistant</a>, as it has similar quantum resistance to AES-128's resistance against traditional, non-quantum, attacks at 128 <a href="/wiki/Bits_of_security" class="mw-redirect" title="Bits of security">bits of security</a>. AES-192 and AES-128 are not considered quantum resistant due to their smaller key sizes. AES-192 has a strength of 96 bits against quantum attacks and AES-128 has 64 bits of strength against quantum attacks, making them both insecure.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="NIST/CSEC_validation"><span id="NIST.2FCSEC_validation"></span>NIST/CSEC validation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: NIST/CSEC validation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/CMVP" class="mw-redirect" title="CMVP">Cryptographic Module Validation Program</a> (CMVP) is operated jointly by the United States Government's <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST) Computer Security Division and the <a href="/wiki/Communications_Security_Establishment" title="Communications Security Establishment">Communications Security Establishment</a> (CSE) of the Government of Canada. The use of cryptographic modules validated to NIST <a href="/wiki/FIPS_140-2" title="FIPS 140-2">FIPS 140-2</a> is required by the United States Government for encryption of all data that has a classification of <a href="/wiki/Sensitive_but_Unclassified" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensitive but Unclassified">Sensitive but Unclassified</a> (SBU) or above. From NSTISSP #11, National Policy Governing the Acquisition of Information Assurance: "Encryption products for protecting classified information will be certified by NSA, and encryption products intended for protecting sensitive information will be certified in accordance with NIST FIPS 140-2."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-cnss.gov&#95;45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnss.gov-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The Government of Canada also recommends the use of <a href="/wiki/FIPS_140" title="FIPS 140">FIPS 140</a> validated cryptographic modules in unclassified applications of its departments.
</p><p>Although NIST publication 197 ("FIPS 197") is the unique document that covers the AES algorithm, vendors typically approach the CMVP under FIPS 140 and ask to have several algorithms (such as <a href="/wiki/Triple_DES" title="Triple DES">Triple DES</a> or <a href="/wiki/SHA1" class="mw-redirect" title="SHA1">SHA1</a>) validated at the same time. Therefore, it is rare to find cryptographic modules that are uniquely FIPS 197 validated and NIST itself does not generally take the time to list FIPS 197 validated modules separately on its public web site. Instead, FIPS 197 validation is typically just listed as an "FIPS approved: AES" notation (with a specific FIPS 197 certificate number) in the current list of FIPS 140 validated cryptographic modules.
</p><p>The Cryptographic Algorithm Validation Program (CAVP)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> allows for independent validation of the correct implementation of the AES algorithm. Successful validation results in being listed on the NIST validations page.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This testing is a pre-requisite for the FIPS 140-2 module validation. However, successful CAVP validation in no way implies that the cryptographic module implementing the algorithm is secure. A cryptographic module lacking FIPS 140-2 validation or specific approval by the NSA is not deemed secure by the US Government and cannot be used to protect government data.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-cnss.gov&#95;45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnss.gov-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>FIPS 140-2 validation is challenging to achieve both technically and fiscally.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-openssl&#95;48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-openssl-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There is a standardized battery of tests as well as an element of source code review that must be passed over a period of a few weeks. The cost to perform these tests through an approved laboratory can be significant (e.g., well over <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>30,000)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-openssl&#95;48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-openssl-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and does not include the time it takes to write, test, document and prepare a module for validation. After validation, modules must be re-submitted and re-evaluated if they are changed in any way. This can vary from simple paperwork updates if the security functionality did not change to a more substantial set of re-testing if the security functionality was impacted by the change.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Test_vectors">Test vectors</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Test vectors"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Test vectors are a set of known ciphers for a given input and key. NIST distributes the reference of AES test vectors as AES Known Answer Test (KAT) Vectors.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>note 5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Performance">Performance</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Encryption_Standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Performance"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>High speed and low RAM requirements were some of the criteria of the AES selection process. As the chosen algorithm, AES performed well on a wide variety of hardware, from 8-bit <a href="/wiki/Smart_card" title="Smart card">smart cards</a> to high-performance computers.
</p><p>On a <a href="/wiki/Pentium_Pro" title="Pentium Pro">Pentium Pro</a>, AES encryption requires 18 clock cycles per byte (cpb),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> equivalent to a throughput of about <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7001110000000000000♠"></span>11&#160;MiB/s</span> for a <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7008200000000000000♠"></span>200&#160;MHz</span> processor.
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Intel_Core" title="Intel Core">Intel Core</a> and <a href="/wiki/AMD_Ryzen" class="mw-redirect" title="AMD Ryzen">AMD Ryzen</a> CPUs supporting <a href="/wiki/AES_instruction_set" title="AES instruction set">AES-NI instruction set</a> extensions, throughput can be multiple GiB/s.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On an Intel <a href="/wiki/Westmere_(microarchitecture)" title="Westmere (microarchitecture)">Westmere</a> CPU, AES encryption using AES-NI takes about <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7000130000000000000♠"></span>1.3&#160;cpb</span> for AES-128, and <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7000180000000000000♠"></span>1.8&#160;cpb</span> for AES-256.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<li id="cite&#95;note-14"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-14">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Large-block variants of Rijndael use an array with additional columns, but always four rows.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite&#95;note-15"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-15">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Rijndael variants with a larger block size have slightly different offsets. For blocks of sizes 128 bits and 192 bits, the shifting pattern is the same. Row <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">n</span> is shifted left circular by <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i>−1</span> bytes.  For a 256-bit block, the first row is unchanged and the shifting for the second, third and fourth row is 1 byte, 3 bytes and 4 bytes respectively—this change only applies for the Rijndael cipher when used with a 256-bit block, as AES does not use 256-bit blocks.</span>
</li>
